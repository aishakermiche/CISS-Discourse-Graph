abstract: "\#\#\# Summary: Magnetoresistance experiment using {dsDNA} and associated tight-binding model, accounting for thermal/vibrational contributions to {SOI}. - Experiment and Results Crossbar-geometry set-up; ferromagnetic electrodes on top only or top \& bottom. Constant current: 0.01mAl; dependent voltage. Magnetic field perpendicularly applied; max strength 0.9T. Self-assembled monolayers used: oligopeptide or {dsDNA} (40bp). Differing optical activities of molecules result in conductance differences. {dsDNA}: Current enhanced when magnetic field and current aligned parallel. Oligo: Current enhanced when magnetic field and current aligned anti-parallel. Spin polarisation increases with temperature. Voltage-independent behaviour. Magnetoresistance increases with temperature. Anti-symmetric pattern for {MR} against field strength. Correlation between spin polarisation and molecule optical activity observed. (but not explicitly tested for in experiment.) - Model Building Single-electron picture, effective-continuum model used to demonstrate how the {SOC} can be expressed to contain two components: a static {SOC} and a first-order correction due to vibration/thermal excitation. (Vibrational contributions do not merely vanish when averaged because resultant nuclear displacements are anharmonic.) Switching to second-quantisation, the authors identify components corresponding to vibrationally-enhanced electron-electron \& effective-exchange interactions. The former is only significant at low temperatures where asymmetry between spin densities is significant. The latter is dependent on energy differences between states, which the authors expect to be the dominant factor at room temperature. The authors build a tight binding model featuring vibrationally-enhanced nearest-neighbour (electron-electron) interactions and next-nearest-neighbour {SOC} (effective-exchange). The model is mounted between one ferromagnetic left-lead and a non-ferromagnetic metallic right-lead. Varying temperature, bias voltage, and spin-polarisation of the left-lead, the following observations were made: 1. Current generally decreases with temperature. 1. Occurs due to spin-dependent dissipation (vibrational {SOC}). Higher temperatures lead to enhanced inelastic scattering. 2. Dependent on spin polarisation sign. On-site with 2nd/4th-neighbour interactions are spin-symmetric (to first order), but 1st with 3rd-neighbour interactions (mixed hopping \& {SOC}) are spin-asymmetric. 3. Zero spin-polarisation in leads results in similar behaviour for both spin-up/down. (Save for very small electron-electron interaction.) 2. Model calculations and experimental results in agreement for spin-polarisation against temperature. 1. Produces similar non-monotonic I-V curve profile as experiments. 2. Model connects spin polarisation to molecular polarisability, hence molecular optical activity. - Proposed Mechanism The chiral molecule is subject to an external electric potential, causing it to become charge-polarised, and hence spin-polarised. Each pole is now correlated to a specific spin-state. A spin blockade arises, due to the Pauli principle, proportional to the singlet-triplet energy gap. Hence, injection of a one-directional current (primarily one spin-state) will see different energy barriers depending on the spin of the electron and the pole into which it enters. This energy difference is proportional to the inner product of spin-polarisation (near the entry pole) and spin-exchange interaction (singlet-triplet conversion) strength. This spin-preference results in current asymmetry depending on the magnetic direction of the electrodes/leads. Spin polarisations are enhanced due to the interface barriers, allowing measured values to exceed those of the lead materials. \#\#\# Comments: - {TODO}: Don't get the bit on the 'larger current {\textbackslash}[will also be{\textbackslash}] injected here' regarding direction of magnetic field (in-plane vs out-of-plane).
