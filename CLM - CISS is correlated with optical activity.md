##### CISS is a soft matter problem
I liked Fransson's comment that when we are dealing with these really soft biomolecules, a single electron makes a huge difference to changing the structure. That is also something that I did not think about. 


My picture is not really novel, it just describes things in a very simple way.  Even though it is technically a single electron picture, my hope is that it allows you to test the particular sources for amplification through smart experimental design.  That's the Its this idea that, specifically from the perspective of electron spin, the electron experiences a pseudo field. This is not a real field, but its effects can "interfere" with other fields in the environment. This comes to mind when you here that CISS is enhanced by circularly polarized light, I think that you can show easily that, and this phrase will come up a lot, **from the electrons perspective**, you can have constructive or destructive interference of the same field. 

My idea is to lump literally everything, multi-electron effects, thermal dissipation, into one amplitude dampening term, see how this changes with other parameters. I think that's the biggest experimental concern. 

Main thesis: no, geometry alone is not sufficient to induce spin polarization in any meaningful way. What it does is **rotate the eigenbasis** so that it can *utilize* amplitude dampening to exaggerate asymmetry. Its not just the mixing of the eigenstates but the *unequal amplitude partitioning* that ensures that asymmetry will be preserved even in the presence of decoherence. The geometry is the "net" that the molecule prepares to catch noise and perturbations so that asymmetry and spin polarization is amplified through repeated relaxation events. 

My picture assumes helical geometry. It doesn't account for CISS that occurs around chiral centers
