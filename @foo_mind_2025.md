### Metascience claims
- Subjecting CISS to spectroscopy or temperature dependence studies is the gold standard. A way for the experimentalists to discriminate between competing mechanisms
- CISS is less a crisis of physics and more a crisis of epistemology
- I'm worried because a lot of the competing claims are not about the empirical reality of the phenomena itself but ontological claims about what is "really going on". I need to distinguish between claims about the reality of the phenomena vs ontological claims about the field. 
- Instead of trying to squeeze a phenomena into an existing formalism and claiming first principles, I think its better to work backwards. Start with fidelity to the mysteries of the phenomena relative to our senses, introduce bare minimum formalism as to make the object usable and testable to an experimentalist. Formalism is for legible containment and translation but its not the point. It never was the point. 
- my idea was always to compressed complex experimental setups into an effective signal, then exploring how different contributions to the effective signal manifest as measurable signatures (i.e. temperature dependence curve) 
- assembling a spin polarization "spectra", with different effective signals at different frequencies 
- the minimal model captures an "antifragility" of a phenomena where random kicks contribute to the underlying asymmetry rather than destroy it
- I'm not sure what "quantumness" means, or preserving quantumness in terms of phase relationships. I remember having an idea from Aharony of doing a kind of interference spectroscopy, to see if phase relationships are somehow preserved.
- What does it mean for a phenomena to be "quantum"
- CISS is at the rich intersection of complexity and causality 
- I really wanted to analyze ciss in terms of resource theories (using Nicole Halpern's formalism. )
- Textbooks get lost on the distinction. Like the quantum and classical regimes are real things rather than choices of description and salience
- Theres a salience problem: what do you want to see, what is important when you have endless detail, what is relevant to the measurement
- Build demonstrations, procedural rhetoric that shows what you believe to be true. 
- Maybe instead of starting from a formalism and squeezing a phenomena into it, we should start with what we want to understand or predict and remove everything not relevant to that goal, and see how it compares to existing formalism
- Its prevents bureaucratic Frankenstein models and cuts through complexity.
- Like my priorities are always 1) fidelity to the mystery of the phenomena 2) making a usable object to the experimentalist. Loyalty to a formalism or method is dead last.
- Also knowing what degrees of freedom actually matter, given the huge diversity of experiments
- Like i think there is one version where only classical or semiclassical transport apply while another scenario where quantum degrees of freedom matter a lot. I think its important to map those two outcomes
- Also, sometimes it matters more that a theory is usable more than if its explicitly true. Parameter fitting is "true", or predicts empirical data well, but it is not usable
- I thought maybe multiway systems from Wolfram would be a nice tool to describe relationships between existing formalisms and experimental outcomes
- I dont believe in the foundational claims i just think its a convenient way to map out a decision tree for an experimentalist
- There's also a confluence of different variables that can converge to the same outcome. I think multiway systems do this well
- Maybe a more precise question: is the mystery of magnitude explictly dependent on the quantum description
- Implication for causality - [Multiway systems as an interventionist map](Multiway%20systems%20as%20an%20interventionist%20map.md)
