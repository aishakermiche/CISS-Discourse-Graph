The problem is discourse graphs are for making an account of empirical claims but not of ontological ones. The problem with CISS is everyone agrees essentially on the empirical observation of the effect, but nobody can agree on the best ontology used to describe the effect. So two bookkeeping tasks are in order. One is mapping the empirical claims into a discourse graph. The other is finding a way to map the ontologies in a way they can be evaluated. 

David Spivak's ologs exist, but they require a level of intricacy and category theory knowledge most people can't access. It's rigor and precision make it illegible for collective use. So we have to develop our own bookkeeping system that lends itself to legibility.

For now what I'll do is divide the claims in the review into empirical claims and ontological claims, and see what is the best way to go about organizing them.

So there are two maps that need to be made. One is - what kind of experiments have we run and what have we observed. What are the unambiguous claims that are well supported by what we have observed. The second map is - ok, so we have a decent map of what we observed. We now need a map of the maps, of how people have tried to put causal or explanation on what we have observed. Seeing these two maps in parallel will help in order to know what kinds of experiments we need in order to choose and utilize the correct ontology of the problem. We also have a deep sense of where the gaps and inconsistencies are, both in what we have observe and what we can and can't explain. I'll probably find out over time how much these two tasks overlap.
